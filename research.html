Distributed Formation

A novel decentralized algorithm can let large numbers of robots form arbitrary repeated lattice pattern, including squares, hexagons, octagon-squares, etc.. Our algorithm takes the desired lattice pattern as input, which is represented as a lattice graph. The robots can autonomously organize themselves into a family of rooted trees, and use these trees to perform task assignments locally without conflicts.

Prior Formation Algorithm

The input to our previous algorithm is a representation of desired formation in the form of a directed graph in which each vertex represents a "role" for a robot to play in the formation, and each outgoing edge is labeled with rigid body transformation indicating the desired location of one of that robot's neighbors.

As the algorithm proceeds, each robot continually broadcasts messages to the other nearby robots. Based on these messages, the robots organize themselves into a rooted tree structure, and then move to the positions assigned to them by their parents in this tree.

New Formation Algorithm

Prior algorithm has two critical limitations:

  1. There is no guarantee that the robots will eventually form the desired formation and terminate the algorithm.
  2. The motion strategy may cause the formation become disconnected, decreasing the overall solution quality.

By defining a new procedure to construct the tree structure and modifying the motion strategy, we prove that robots executing this new algorithm will form the desired lattice with bounded execution time.

Geometric Planning

By using a geometric approximation method to represent the robot's states and uncertainties, we constrained the true information state (I-state) to remain in a structured subset of the I-space.

The experimental results for a sensor-based navigation task demostrated that using our method, compared to a high-fidelity representation of the un-approximated I-state, achieves a similar success rate at a small fraction of the computational cost.
